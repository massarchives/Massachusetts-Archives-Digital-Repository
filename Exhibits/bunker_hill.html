Affidavit of Francis Davidson of Londonderry, NH that he fought and was wounded at Bunker Hill

Davidson claims he was stationed at “the Rail fence and at that place I received my wound and was carried from that place as I was afterwards informed having fainted with the loss of blood.” Accompanied by an attestation by Samuel Amos, Justice of the Peace, and a request for Davidson to be paid seven dollars for attendance and travel to the celebration.

Letter from Nathan Fisher of Westborough to William H. Sumner, Adjutant General, containing a detailed account of his participation at the Battle of Bunker Hill, as any other person who could certify his participation “are dead and gone,” June 28, 1825

Fisher writes that on the way to Bunker Hill, “two flooding batteries & … two sloops of war were continually cannonading us,” and that as the British soldiers approached, “we fired upon them & put them into great confusion & when the wind blew the smoak away we could see may lay flat on their backs we supposed them dead – it was some little time before they could form their line again.” He also describes the final engagement, where “no doubt we killed many of the poor fellows they being perhaps not more than 6 or 8 rods off [100–130 feet], and many of us fired two balls each, & one ball & 3 or 4 buck shot. after this fire we was ordered to retreat as fast as possible.” After his description of the battle, Fisher requests payment for his attendance and travel to the celebration of the battle.

Testimony of Elias Pike of Newburyport that he attended the celebration of the battle, and that on June 17, 1775, he “was in the Fort on Bunkers or Breeds, in the memorable battle which was fought with the British troops on that day,” and “that during the engagement … he received a wound from a musket ball of the enemy, which entered the left thigh.” Sworn before William Woast, Notary Public, June 30, 1825
